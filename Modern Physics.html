Electromagnetic Induction

Electromagnetic Induction

Inducing an electromotive force by changing the magnetic flux in a closed loop.

Faraday’s Law

The size of the induced emf is directly proportional to the rate of change of flux.

Lenz’s Law

The direction of an induced current is always such as to oppose the change producing it.

Electrical Generator

Uses electromagnetic induction to convert mechanical energy to electrical energy.

Mutual Induction

When a changing magnetic field in one coil causes an induced emf to appear in a nearby coil.

Transformer

Device that changes the value of an alternating voltage.

Self-Induction

Occurs when a changing magnetic field in a coil induces an emf in the coil itself.

Magnets and Magnetic Fields

Magnetic Field

A region of space where magnetic forces can be felt.

Magnetic Field Line

A tangent on any point of a magnetic field line gives the magnetic field direction at this point.

Right-Hand Grip Rule

If the right hand grasps a wire with the thumb pointing in the direction of the current, the fingers coiled around the wire show the magnetic field direction around it.

Fleming’s Left-Hand Rule

If the thumb, index finger and middle finger are all perpendicular to each other, with the index finger pointing in the direction of the magnetic field and the middle finger pointing in the direction of the current, then the thumb points in the direction of the force.

Magnetic Flux Density (B)

A vector at any point in the magnetic field whose magnitude is equivalent to the force experienced by a conductor of a length of 1 m, carrying a current of 1 A, at right angles to the field at that point and has the same direction as the magnetic field lines.

Tesla

Unit of magnetic flux density. Magnetic flux density at a point is 1 if a 1 m long conductor carrying a current of 1 A experiences a force of 1 N when placed perpendicular to the field.

Weber

Unit of magnetic flux. The magnetic flux over 1 m² is 1 Wb if placed in a field of magnetic flux density 1 T.

Electromagnet

Made of a solenoid and a soft iron core. When current passes though the solenoid, the core becomes magnetic.

Ampere

That constant current which, if maintained in two straight parallel conductors of infinite length, of negligible cross section and placed 1 m apart in a vacuum, would produce a force on each conductor of 2 x 10⁻⁷ newtons per meter of length.

Nuclear Energy

Nuclear Fission

The splitting up of a large nucleus into two smaller nuclei of similar size with the release of energy and neutrons.

Nuclear Fusion

The joining of two smaller nuclei to form a larger nucleus with the release of energy.

Moderator

Usually graphite or heavy water, it is used in a nuclear reactor to slow down neutrons so that they can cause fission reactions.

Control Rods

Used in a nuclear reactor to absorb neutrons and therefore control the rate of reaction.

The Atom, Nucleus & Radioactivity

Emission Spectrum

Created when light from a luminous source is dispersed.

Atomic Number (Z)

The number of protons in an element’s nucleus.

Mass Number (A)

The total number of protons and neutrons in the nucleus of an atom of a certain element.

Isotopes

Atoms that have the same atomic number but different mass numbers because of more/less neutrons present in the nucleus.

Radioactivity

The spontaneous disintegration or decay of the nucleus of certain atoms with the emission of one or more types of radiation.

Energy Level

A fixed quantity of energy which an electron in an atom can have.

Alpha (α) Radiation

Fast-moving helium nuclei ejected from the nuclei of radioactive atoms.

Beta (β) Radiation

High speed electrons ejected from the nuclei of radioactive atoms.

Gamma (γ) Radiation

High frequency electromagnetic radiation emitted from the nuclei of radioactive atoms.

Ionisation

When an atom or molecule acquires a charge by gaining or losing electrons to form ions.

Activity

The number of nuclei of a substance decaying per second.

Becquerel

The unit of activity equivalent to 1 disintegration per second.

Law of Radioactive Decay

The activity of a sample is directly proportional to the amount left undecayed.

Half-Life

The time taken for half a sample’s present atoms to decay.

Cosmic Rays

High-energy particles that originate in very distant parts of the universe. They mostly decay high in the Earth’s atmosphere.

The Electron

Electron

Sub-atomic negatively-charged particle that orbits the nucleus.

Thermionic Emission

When electrons are released from the surface of a hot metal.

Cathode Rays

Streams of extremely fast electrons traveling from the cathode to the anode in an evacuated tube.

The Electronvolt (eV)

The energy gained/lost by an electron as it moves through a p.d. of 1 V. Its value is 1.6 x 10⁻¹⁹ J.

Photoelectric Effect

The release of electrons from a metal surface when EM radiation of above a certain frequency falls on it.

Photon

A packet of electromagnetic energy. Its energy is dependent on its frequency.

Threshold Frequency

The minimum frequency needed for photoemission to occur. Every metal has a unique value.

Work Function

The minimum energy needed by a photon to remove an electron from the surface of a metal.

X-Rays

High frequency EM radiation produced when high speed electrons strike a metal target.
