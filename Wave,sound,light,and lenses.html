Light

Light

Electromagnetic radiation that is detectable by the human eye. Wavelength: 400nm-700nm.

Self-Luminous

An object that produces its own light, e.g. the Sun.

Non-Luminous

An object that does not produce its own light and so reflects light from its surface, making it visible.

Dispersion

The separation of the different wavelengths/colours present in light.

Spectrometer

Instrument used in optics to examine spectra and measure the wavelength of light.

Primary Colours

These form white light when combined. They are red, blue and green.

Secondary Colours

These are formed by mixing two primary colours in equal intensity. They are yellow, magenta and cyan.

Complementary Colours

A primary colour and a secondary colour that form white when they are combined. These are blue with yellow, green with magenta and red with cyan.

Infrared Light

Light that is emitted by warm objects.

Fluorescence

When a body absorbs UV radiation and re-emits it as visible light.

Monochromatic Light

Light of one wavelength/colour only.

Reflection, Refraction, Mirrors and Lenses

Laws of Reflection

1. The incident ray, the reflected ray, and the normal at the point of incidence all lie in the same plane.
2. The angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection.

Real Image

Image formed by the actual intersection of rays.

Virtual Image

Image formed by the apparent intersection of rays.

Parallax

The difference in apparent position of an object when viewed from different positions.

Refraction

The bending of light as it passes from one medium to another.

Laws of Refraction

1. The incident ray, the refracted ray, and the normal all lie in the same plane.
2. For a given pair of media, the sine of the angle of incidence is proportional to the sine of the angle of refraction (Snell’s law).

Refractive Index

The ratio of the sine of the angle of incidence to the sine of the angle of refraction when light travels from air into the medium.

Critical Angle

The angle of incidence in the denser medium when the angle of refraction in the rarer medium is 90°.

Total Internal Reflection

This occurs when the angle of incidence in the denser medium is larger than the critical angle so the light is reflected back into the denser medium.

Optic Fibre

Very thin, long, and transparent material through which light travels by total internal reflection.

Accommodation

The ability of the eye to focus on objects at varying distances by changing the shape of the lens.

Power of a Lens

The reciprocal of the lens focal length, i.e., 1/f.

Magnification

The ratio of the image distance/height to the object distance/height.

Vibrations and Sound

Sound

Mechanical waves produced by a vibrating object, e.g., tuning fork.

Acoustics

The study of sound.

Fundamental Frequency

The frequency at which a body tends to vibrate if free to do so.

Harmonics

Frequencies that are multiples of the fundamental frequency. If f = fundamental frequency, then f = 1st harmonic.

Overtones

Frequencies that are multiples of the fundamental frequency. If f = fundamental frequency, then 2f = 1st overtone.

Resonance

When a body is forced to vibrate at its own natural frequency, resulting in large amplitude oscillations.

The Threshold of Hearing

The smallest intensity audible by the human ear at a frequency of 1000 Hz. It has a value of 1x10-12 Wm⁻².

Sound Intensity

The power carried by sound waves per unit area in a direction perpendicular to that area.

Sound Intensity Level

Measured in decibels, it gives the intensity of a sound relative to the threshold of hearing.

Waves and Wave Motion

Wave

A disturbance that transfers energy through a medium, without any net movement of the medium.

Medium

Required to carry a wave, with the exception of electromagnetic waves.

Mechanical Waves

Waves which require a medium to travel.

Electromagnetic Waves

Waves which do not require a medium in which to travel. EM waves travel in a vacuum at the speed of light.

Electromagnetic Spectrum

The range of EM waves. From lowest to highest frequency we have radio waves, microwaves, infrared, visible light, ultraviolet, x-rays and gamma rays.

Transverse Wave

A wave whose vibrations are perpendicular to the direction in which the wave is travelling, e.g., light waves.

Longitudinal Wave

A wave whose vibrations are parallel to the direction in which the wave is travelling, e.g., sound waves.

Travelling Wave

Transfers energy as it travels from the source, producing it to all areas which it passes.

Reflection

The bouncing of a wave off the surface of obstacles in its path.

Refraction

The change in direction in which a wave is travelling when it travels from one medium to another.

Coherent Sources

Sources that are in phase and have the same frequency.

Interference

Occurs when two or more waves meet. The resulting disturbance has an amplitude that is the algebraic sum of the individual amplitudes of each interfering wave.

Interference Pattern

Forms when two (or more) coherent sources meet.

Constructive Interference

Occurs when two waves meet and the amplitude of the resultant wave is greater than the amplitude of each individual wave.

Destructive Interference

Occurs when two waves meet and the amplitude of the resultant wave is less than the amplitude of each individual wave.

Diffraction

The sideways spreading of waves into the region after passing through/around a small gap/obstacle.

Polarisation

When the vibrations of a wave are confined to one plane only.

Stationary/Standing

Formed when two waves meet which are travelling in opposite directions but have the same amplitude and frequency.

Wave Nodes

Points along a stationary wave which experience no amplitude.

Antinodes

Points along a stationary wave which experience maximum vibration and amplitude.

Doppler Effect

The apparent change in frequency of a wave due to the relative motion of the source or the observer.

Wavelength

The distance between two successive crests or troughs.

Frequency

The number of complete oscillations of a wave per second.

Velocity

The distance travelled by a wave per second.

Amplitude

The maximum displacement of a point on a wave from the mean position.

Periodic Time

The time taken for one complete oscillation of a wave.
