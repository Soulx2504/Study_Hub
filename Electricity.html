Current and Charge

Insulator

A substance through which electric charge cannot flow.

Conductor

A substance through which electric charge can flow.

Current

The rate of flow of charge.

Ammeter

Device that measures current.

Alternating Current (AC)

Current that constantly changes direction.

Direct Current (DC)

Current that flows in one direction only.

Rectification

The conversion of alternating current (AC) to direct current (DC).

Ion

An atom/group of atoms that have lost or gained one or more electrons.

Electrolysis

Causing a chemical reaction by passing a current through a liquid.

Electric Circuits

Series

A circuit where there is no split.

Parallel

A circuit where there is a split.

Fuse

A safety device consisting of a wire which melts when a current above a pre-set value passes through it, interrupting the circuit and preventing electrocution.

Miniature Circuit Breaker (MCB)

A safety device which breaks a circuit if a current above a pre-set value is detected.

Residual Current Device (RCD)

A safety device which breaks a circuit if a current difference between live and neutral above a pre-set value is detected.

Radial Circuit

One where a separate live and neutral wire are connected from the distribution box to an appliance which uses a large amount of current, e.g. an electric shower.

Ring Circuit

One where the live, neutral and earth terminal of each socket are connected to three corresponding wires arranged in a loop with each end of said loop connected to the distribution box.

Bonding

A safety precaution whereby all metal pipes, taps and tanks are earthed as a safety precaution.

Kilowatt-Hour

The amount of energy used per hour by a 1000 W appliance.

Potential Difference & Capacitance

Potential Difference

The work done in bringing unit positive charge from one point to another.

Coulomb

Unit of electric charge. It is the quantity of charge that passes when a current of 1 A flows for 1 second.

Volt

The potential difference between two points is 1 volt if 1 joule of work is done in bringing 1 coulomb from one point to another.

Capacitance

The ratio of charge to potential in a conductor.

Electromotive Force (EMF)

Potential difference between terminals of a battery when no current is being drawn from the battery.

Voltmeter

Device that measures potential difference across a component or a circuit.

Resistance

Resistance

The ratio of the voltage across a conductor to the current flowing through it.

Ohm

Unit of resistance. A conductor has 1 ohm of resistance if a current of 1 A passes through it when a potential difference of 1 V is applied across it.

Ohm’s Law

If the temperature remains constant, the current flowing through a conductor is directly proportional to the potential difference across it.

Resistivity

The potential a material has for resistance.

Joule’s Law

The rate of heat produced in a conductor is proportional to the square of the current flowing through it: P ∝ I².

Thermistor

A semiconductor whose resistance decreases as the temperature increases.

Light Dependent Resistor (LDR)

A semiconductor whose resistance decreases as light intensity increases.

Semiconductors

Semiconductor

A substance whose resistivity is between that of a good conductor and that of a good insulator.

Holes

Positively charged spaces in a substance which remain when electrons break free from a covalent bond.

Doping

Increasing the conductivity of a semiconductor by the addition of impurities.

Intrinsic Conduction

Occurs in pure semiconductors due to electrons moving from negative to positive and an equal number of holes moving in the opposite direction.

Extrinsic Conduction

Increased conduction in a semiconductor due to the addition of impurities.

Wheatstone Bridge

A circuit used to find an unknown value of a resistor through the use of ratios.

P-Type Semiconductor

One in which the impurity added produces extra holes which are available for conduction, e.g. adding boron to silicon.

N-Type Semiconductor

One in which the impurity added produces more free electrons available for conduction, e.g. adding phosphorous to silicon.

P-N Junction

When a P and an N type semiconductor are joined together they form a single semiconductor which allows current to flow in one direction only.

Depletion Layer

The insulative region at the meeting of the p-n junction where there are no majority charge carriers.

Junction Voltage

The potential difference across a p-n junction caused by holes and electrons moving across the junction when it was formed.

Forward Biased P-N Junction

Positive terminal of battery to p-type and negative terminal of battery to n-type. Conducts electricity.

Reverse Biased P-N Junction

Positive terminal of battery to n-type and negative terminal of battery to p-type. Does not conduct electricity.

Light Emitting Diode (LED)

A diode that emits light when in forward bias.

Static Electricity

Electrostatics

Properties of charged particles when they are at rest.

Negative Charge

The charge an object has when it has more electrons than protons.

Positive Charge

The charge an object has when it has more protons than electrons.

Coulomb’s Law

The force between two point charges is directly proportional to the product of their charges and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them.

Electric Field

A region of space where a positive electric charge experiences a force other than gravity.

Electric Field Strength

The force per unit charge at a certain point in an electric field.

Electric Field Line

The line along which a positive charge would travel in an electric field.

Work, Energy and Power

Work

Done when a force causes a body to be moved.

Energy

The ability to do work.

Potential Energy

Stored energy that a body can have due to its state or position.

Kinetic Energy

Energy a body can have due to its motion.

Joule (Unit)

1 Joule is the work done when an object is displaced by 1 meter.

The Principle Of Conservation Of Energy

Energy can neither be created nor destroyed, but can change from one form to another.

Power

The rate at which is work is done. OR The rate at which energy is converted.

Watt (Unit)

Doing work equal to 1 Joule per second is the rate of 1 Watt of power.

Renewable Energy

Produced by a source which will not run out or be exhausted, eg wind, solar, tidal.

Non-Renewable Energy

Produced by a source which will eventually run out, eg oil, coal, natural gas.

Machine Efficiency

Energy output as a percentage of energy input.
